Project Overview

What is Serenia?

Serenia is a small conceptual smart city model that I designed as part of my DAA course project. The main idea is to take the algorithms from the DAA syllabus and connect them to different parts of a city such as roads, services, and resource management.

I divided the city into zones like residential area, commercial zone, hospital, government offices, tech park, green belt, and transport hub. After deciding the zones, I related each of them to suitable algorithms such as Dijkstra’s, BFS, DFS, Minimum Spanning Tree, greedy methods, and dynamic programming.

I referred to online resources for better understanding and used ChatGPT mainly for clarification and formatting support. The planning, sketches, layout decisions and explanations were done by me based on my understanding of the course.

Algorithm Mapping

How Algorithms Fit into Serenia

The main goal of this project was to connect DAA concepts to real-world city planning. Below are some of the algorithms from the syllabus and how I imagined them being used inside Serenia.

1. Dijkstra’s Algorithm

Used to model shortest and fastest routes inside the city.

  • Finding quickest path to the hospital.
  • Routing between residential and commercial zones.
  • Reducing overall travel time in the road network.

2. BFS

Used for reachability and coverage analysis of services.

  • Checking if all zones receive water supply.
  • Planning waste collection coverage.
  • Ensuring basic city services are connected.

3. DFS

Used for deeper path exploration in limited regions.

  • Scanning CCTV routes in public spaces.
  • Exploring building-wise security checks.
  • Inspecting internal paths or escape routes.

4. Minimum Spanning Tree

Used to think about cost-effective connections.

  • Planning streetlight connections.
  • Laying power lines between zones.
  • Reducing total wiring cost.

5. Greedy Approach

Used where quick local decisions are needed.

  • Choosing next dustbin or area for garbage pickup.
  • Selecting roads for patrolling first.
  • Handling simple decisions in peak hours.

6. Divide and Conquer

Used to conceptually split the city into manageable zones.

  • Planning one region at a time.
  • Managing local traffic separately per zone.
  • Reducing the complexity of overall design.

7. Dynamic Programming

Used for repeated decisions and limited resources.

  • Allocating beds and staff in the hospital.
  • Managing limited medical supplies.
  • Handling multiple emergencies in an organised way.

8. Sorting

Used to keep city data neat and searchable.

  • Sorting property records by zone.
  • Arranging vendor and shop lists.
  • Ordering complaints or service requests.

9. Hashing

Used for fast lookups in records.

  • Quick citizen or ID verification.
  • Vehicle number checking at points.
  • Maintaining fast-access city records.
Course Context

About DAA and the Lab

Design and Analysis of Algorithms

Design and Analysis of Algorithms (DAA) is a subject that teaches how to solve problems efficiently. It focuses on how an algorithm behaves for different inputs, how much time it takes, how much memory it uses, and why one method can be better than another.

In this course we learn time and space complexity, recursion, divide and conquer, greedy methods, dynamic programming, hashing, searching, sorting and basic graph algorithms. These topics form an important part of problem-solving in computer science.

Lab Work and Project

In the DAA lab, we implement many of these algorithms as programs and observe how they behave with different kinds of input. We work with searching, sorting, tree operations, graph traversal and shortest path problems.

As part of the lab, I chose to design Serenia as my course project. Mapping the algorithms to a smart city model helped me clearly understand where these concepts can appear in real situations instead of only in theory or coding problems.
